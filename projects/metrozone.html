A city builder that keeps moving

MetroZone is designed around the tension between planning and response. You choose where roads, zones, parks, factories, highways, and apartments go; the game then turns those decisions into income, population, happiness, crime, traffic, and the next constraint you need to solve.

Growth

Five city scales

Progress from Village through Metropolis as the city reaches new population and development thresholds, unlocking a broader palette of things to build.

Economy

Numbers with consequences

The income panel separates residential, commercial, industrial, service, and expense effects so a growing city can still be a costly one.

Pressure

Happiness and crime

Population is not the only score. City wellbeing and crime make parks, services, and the mix of development matter.

Systems built for compounding decisions

The simulation is split into legible systems instead of treating a city as a static collection of models. A road network lets districts connect; traffic follows the network; economy ticks convert the city state into cash; and events inject a reason to revisit an established plan.

SystemImplementation focusPlayer-facing result
Roads & highwaysAuto-connecting arms, inclined ramps, 2×2 roundaboutsA road layout that has visual and routing meaning.
TrafficRight-hand lanes and per-car slot reservationCars move without stacking into one another.
EconomyPeriodic income, costs, growth, and breakdown UIThe build menu connects to readable financial feedback.
EventsWeighted earthquake, UFO, tornado, fire, festival, crime wave, and moreUnexpected setbacks and opportunities in a city that would otherwise stabilize.
Why the event layer matters: it turns a perfect grid into a plan that must survive. A well-run city still has to react when the ground shakes, a fire starts, or a festival changes the balance overnight.

Procedural 3D without a model pipeline

MetroZone uses Three.js and procedural geometry rather than external asset packs. That keeps the city visually readable while allowing the world to be created from the same underlying grid that drives the simulation.

Buildings

Residential, commercial, industrial, parks, and services are generated as part of the browser world instead of imported as a scene.

Light

A four-minute day/night cycle interpolates sky colour, ambient light, and window glow across the city.

Camera

Zoom, rotation, and map navigation support both the macro decision and close observation of the city it produces.

Traffic understands lanes, road layers, and occupancy

The 402-line traffic module is not a decorative spline. Cars originate from houses, find adjacent drivable cells, reserve direction-specific slots, drive on the right, choose legal exits from connection masks, and change elevation on bridge ramps.

Slot reservation

A cell-direction key prevents multiple cars claiming the same lane slot.

Layer-aware bridges

Ground traffic stays on under-road strips while deck traffic climbs to a calculated 0.97-unit surface.

Roundabout motion

Cars follow a 1.23-unit ring midpoint rather than cutting through the island.

Day-linked density

The car cap falls with night factor while emissive lights strengthen.

Home departure

A half-second transition connects the house to its road cell.

Connection masks

N/E/S/W bits define legal movement through every junction type.

Road geometry rebuilds around neighbours

Plain roads toggle directional asphalt arms and markings from the connection mask. Corners suppress straight lines; T-junctions receive stubs; four-way intersections add yellow approaches and white crosswalks. Bridges and roundabouts regenerate when nearby roads change.

The art stack is generated in code

The 1,054-line building module assembles structures, roads, bridges, roundabouts, street details, smoke, windows, and animations from Three.js primitives. Road lamps avoid scene-wide point lights; tagged window materials let the day/night system update emissive intensity in batches.

One source of truth: the simulation grid and visual city stay aligned because connection geometry and lighting are derived from live state.

Runtime model

A frame loop with slower civic rhythms

MetroZone updates camera interpolation, animated ground, vehicles, active events and lighting every frame. Civic accounting runs every five seconds, while the four-minute environmental cycle advances the in-world clock and day counter.

CadenceWorkEffect
Every frameClamp delta time; update camera, ground, traffic, event actors, lighting and renderer.Movement remains independent from economic balance.
Every five secondsScan cells, total population and income, charge $100 plus $5 per building, update risks and ease happiness 20% toward target.Numbers change in legible steps instead of flickering.
Across 240 secondsMove the sun, interpolate sky colors and lights, derive night factor and roll the day at midnight.One clock controls atmosphere, progression and headlights.
On progressEvaluate the next level’s day or population requirement and unlock its building set once.Progression follows city outcomes rather than separate XP.

Grid authority

3D buildings obey exact cell rules

The visible city is Three.js geometry, but the simulation’s source of truth is a 40×40 map keyed by integer coordinates. Each four-unit cell translates to and from world space, keeping picking, roads, traffic and economy in agreement.

Footprints

Definitions may occupy several cells. Placement checks the full footprint and centers multi-cell geometry.

Road access

Thirteen residential, civic, commercial and industrial types require adjacent transport before placement.

Facing

Homes, shops, offices, apartments and key services rotate toward a neighboring road automatically.

Replacement

Road tooling understands roads, bridges and roundabouts, rebuilding neighbors so markings remain truthful.

Bulldozing

Removal clears cells and scene objects, then refreshes nearby road geometry. It intentionally gives no refund.

Shared state

Economy, levels, events and traffic iterate the same records; visuals cannot drift from simulation.

Environmental system

Day and night change more than the sky

A full day lasts four real minutes. The module advances normalized time, interpolates sky-color stops, rotates the sun and balances directional, ambient and hemisphere light.

Daylight runs from roughly 05:00 to 19:00. Outside that interval a night factor rises and the main loop applies it to building windows. Vehicle meshes retain headlight and taillight materials so emissive intensity can rise after dark. The UI derives hours and minutes from the same clock, and midnight increments the game day used by progression.

One clock, several consumers: atmosphere, windows, vehicle lights, displayed time and the first level unlock all use the same temporal state.

Player interaction

An isometric camera backed by precise picking

The camera keeps a 48-degree elevation while allowing rotation, distance zoom and pan. Current values ease toward goals, giving smooth movement without making grid selection imprecise.

Screen-relative pan

WASD and arrows use camera-relative vectors, so controls remain intuitive after rotation.

Zoom-aware speed

Pan scale grows with distance: whole-city travel is practical while close placement stays controlled.

Raycast picking

A screen ray hits the ground; world position then becomes a grid cell or an out-of-map rejection.

Contextual hover

The game distinguishes occupied, replaceable, unaffordable, locked and road-starved targets before clicks.

Definition-driven dock

Category metadata builds the tray; cards expose level, cost, income and population information.

Six-step tutorial

Onboarding introduces movement, roads, homes, economy, expansion and events while play is paused.

Status

MetroZone is live on Vercel and can be played directly in the browser. The project is currently a static, no-build-step game; the natural next milestones are local save/load, a minimap for large cities, and additional landmark-scale building types.
